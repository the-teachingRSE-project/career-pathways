# Thematic Summary of RSE Promotion Practices

This summary clusters workshop responses on how Research Software Engineering (RSE) is or should be promoted within organizations. It distinguishes between intended (desired) practices and explicitly stated current practices.

## 1. Visibility and Communication

RSE promotion relies heavily on making the topic visible across the organization.

**Desired state:**
- Regular communication channels (newsletters, email, wiki)
- Events such as workshops, tutorials, and community days
- Clear role denomination (“RSE” as a recognized term)
- Cross-institutional visibility

**Observed practices:**
- Often dependent on individuals promoting RSE informally
- In some cases, no visibility at all (“not at all”, “never heard of it”)

Key insight: Visibility is often ad hoc rather than strategically managed.

---

## 2. Community and Skills Development

RSE is strongly tied to knowledge exchange and training.

**Desired state:**
- Structured training, tutorials, and certifications
- Community events (e.g., developer days)
- Active networks and communities of practice

**Observed practices:**
- Individual-driven initiatives
- Some isolated examples of events or active members

Key insight: Community structures exist, but are not consistently established.

---

## 3. Institutionalization and Strategic Embedding

A central theme is the need to embed RSE within formal organizational structures. 

**Desired state:**
- Dedicated RSE groups or departments
- Integration into policies (e.g., Open Science, Open Source)
- Bundling of RSE-related projects
- Clear organizational responsibilities

**Observed practices:**
- Fragmented groups without overarching strategy
- Predominantly bottom-up initiatives
- Some examples of formal groups or clusters

Key insight: There is a gap between grassroots activity and stable institutional structures.

---

## 4. Recognition and Career Pathways

This is one of the most significant gaps identified. See chapter 3 of the six of the [FutuRSI paving the way paper](https://zenodo.org/records/17672125).

**Desired state:**
- Defined career paths for RSEs
- Long-term or permanent positions
- Recognition of software as a research output
- Awards, funding opportunities, and publication venues

**Observed practices:**
- Partial support from supervisors
- Lack of long-term career perspectives and stability

Key insight: Recognition mechanisms are widely desired but rarely implemented structurally.

---

## 5. Integration into Research and Infrastructure

RSE should be embedded as a core component of research processes. See chapter 6 of the six of the [FutuRSI paving the way paper](https://zenodo.org/records/17672125).

**Desired state:**
- Recognition of software as both infrastructure and research output
- Strong integration with HPC and IT services
- Proactive support for researchers

**Observed practices:**
- Often framed as a support service
- Interaction with users mainly operational (e.g., HPC support)

Key insight: RSE is frequently positioned as a service rather than an equal research contribution.

---

## 6. Perception and Cultural Challenges

Cultural perception plays a critical role in how RSE is adopted.

**Desired state:**
- RSE as a valued and integral part of research
- Positive and attractive positioning

**Observed practices:**
- Perceived as a “necessary evil”
- Low awareness or unclear relevance

Key insight: Cultural acceptance remains a major barrier.

---

## Overall Conclusion

The responses indicate a strong emphasis on the desired future state of RSE promotion. Current practices are often:

- Fragmented
- Driven by individuals rather than institutions
- Operational rather than strategic, 

The main gap lies between active efforts (communication, events, training) and the lack of structural embedding (career paths, recognition, institutional strategy).
